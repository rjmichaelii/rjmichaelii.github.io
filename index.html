History

Big Game Alaska was founded in 1993 by Mike Miller with a few plains, bison, and Rocky Mountain elk. In the last 30 years, Alaska Wildlife Conservation Center has transformed into a 501(c) 3 nonprofit status in 2000. In 2007 the name Big Game Alaska was changed from the one known today as AWCC. Located at Mile 79 of the Seward Highway, this 200-acre sanctuary is the number one visitor attraction in Southcentral Alaska and is home to various animals. This sanctuary includes lynx, bears, porcupines, wood bison, foxes, wolves, moose, elk, and more! In a joint effort since 2003, the AWCC and the Alaska Department of Fish and Game have been participating in a program to reintroduce native wood bison back into Alaska after 100 years of absence.

Education

Educating is an essential part of the Alaska Wildlife Conservation Center’s mission. They are constantly working to provide opportunities for school groups and guests throughout the year. They help hundreds of visitors daily through field trips, presentations, lessons, internships tours, programs, art contests, and virtually. They recently received a $250,000 grant from Matson to help with an Ocean Education Center. With this, in the Fall of 2023, they plan to build the Maston Education Center where AWCC meets the waters of Turnagain Arm. Here Cook Inlet beluga whales can be seen as well as icebergs, bore tides, and glaciers allowing AWCC to explain their education programs further.

Goals

Today Alaska Wildlife Conservation Center has ever-increasing demands for animal care, both in resources and medical, and is looking to expand and improve its facilities. With this expansion comes opportunities; however, options require housing. To improve, AWCC wants to create a more efficient kitchen, collocate an animal infirmary, gear, workstations, restrooms, a break room, and overnight accommodations. Girdwood is the closest location with this solution, but AWCC needs to offer onsite accommodation for their employees because of the unaffordable rates.
